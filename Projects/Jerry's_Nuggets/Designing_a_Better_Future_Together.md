Somewhere around 2012, it dawned on me that many of my longtime role models and inspirations ‚Äî the people who innovated the various systems I just listed (and [many more](https://bra.in/4pxQ56)) ‚Äî had several profound things in common. 

First, I realized that many (most?) of my role models were contrarians. Each of them was carrying a message difficult enough for their peers to hear that they were often marginalized or ridiculed by their peers. Their insights ran against their discipline's standing conventional wisdom. Give people with no collateral loans? No way! Let any fool on the planet edit the Wikipedia? Are you nuts? Remove traffic lights? Seriously? Let any worker stop the assembly line? We'll never get anything built! 

You see what I mean. 

Then I discovered the thread that connected their initiatives, which I called Design from Trust. 

 Any system that has more than two humans in it involves trust, which has countless facets. The facet I'm focusing on here isn't building high-trust teams, or using transparency to increase trust, or revealing defects in a system to raise long-term trust. Instead, I've been fascinated by a more specific take on trust that has to do with systems design. 
 
 What's crazy about it is that redesigning the system in this way then affects many of the other facets. Letting anyone stop the assembly line builds high-trust teams and improves product quality. 

**The tl;dr:** Design from Trust redesigns systems from an assumption that most people who will touch the system are good actors. They want to enjoy the system, and may even wish to improve it. 

Instead of designing out all risks, DfT lets risk engage participants and inspire them to contribute significantly to the systems' functioning. If pedestrians, bicyclists and drivers don't slow down, make eye contact, and pace match through intersections, Traffic Calming fails. If nobody steps forward at an Unconference and names a topic for conversation, there won't be any sessions.

## History 

Believe it or not, my catalyst for these insights was the single word "[consumer](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4VfeuITmNUk)." 

[Somehow](https://wiki.openglobalmind.com/projects/jerry's_nuggets/my_story_of_trust), over the past multiple decades (and centuries), we lost faith in humans. Then we designed all our institutions as if everyone was a bad actor. 

What's worse, we adapted, we got used to it. We take mistrust completely for granted now. We've normalized it so well that mistrust is the default setting and we're startled when that setting switches. 

This wasn't always so. Long ago, we figured out [[We Used to Know How to Live in Community on the Commons|how to live in community, on the Commons]]. It was hard-won wisdom, and we've forgotten most of it.
