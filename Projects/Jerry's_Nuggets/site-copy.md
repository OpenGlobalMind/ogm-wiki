  Not as a hobby. As a practice. What started as a personal
  knowledge graph has become — after 28 years and over half a
  million linked nodes — a living map of everything I've read,
  thought, questioned, and connected.

  Every book, person, idea, technology, failure, and question
  I've encountered is in there, linked to everything else I know.
  When I bring it into a room, the room changes. That's not a
  feature. That's the point.

  sensemaking — live, collaborative, and steered entirely by what
  your audience actually cares about. No slides. No script. Just a

  surprises people isn't that I know things. It's that everything

  they think about their own.

## HOW IT WORKS SECTION

Section Label (IBM Plex Mono, Teal):
  Sense-Making, Live

Section Heading (Bebas Neue, Cream):
  How a Session Works

Intro text (Syne, Mid):
  Each session is live improvisation — structured enough to be
  coherent, open enough to be surprising. The audience steers everything.

Steps:

  01 / 05  THE CHALLENGE
  Glyph: ?
  Chat opens. Audience submits topics — anything from "large language
  models" to "why Blockbuster failed" to "what is trust, actually."
  The more real the question, the better.

  02 / 05  THE NAVIGATION
  Glyph: ◎
  Jerry types the topic into the Brain live on screen. The graph
  expands. He narrates what he's seeing — parent concepts, unexpected
  neighbors, questions the Brain raises back.

  03 / 05  THE RABBIT HOLE
  Glyph: ↗
  The audience steers — deeper, sideways, or to a new challenge
  entirely. Jerry follows. The best sessions visit 5–7 topics and
  end somewhere nobody expected.

  04 / 05  THE WEAVE
  Glyph: ∿
  As threads accumulate, patterns emerge. Jerry names them —
  connections between the session's topics that reveal something
  about the room's collective thinking.

  05 / 05  THE SHAPE
  Glyph: ◈
  Closing 5 minutes. Jerry names what the session's shape reveals
  about the conference theme or this particular moment in time.
  Always different. Always worth having.

## FAQ SECTION

Section Label (IBM Plex Mono, Ember):
  Questions

Sidebar Heading (Bebas Neue):
  Still Wondering?

Sidebar Note (Syne, Mid):
  If your question isn't here, it probably deserves a node of its
  own. Reach out — Jerry replies personally.

Q1: This sounds like a game show. Is it?
A1: The dare format is the door — it creates permission to ask
    anything and makes the whole thing feel alive. But what's behind
    the door isn't trivia. It's 28 years of serious thinking about
    how ideas connect. The game-show energy gets people in the room.
    The sensemaking is why they stay.

Q2: Why is there a surcharge for Microsoft Teams?
A2: Teams' screen-sharing architecture introduces real latency to
    TheBrain's live navigation — the visual responsiveness that makes
    the session feel genuinely live is diminished. The $1,000
    surcharge reflects extra technical prep and cognitive overhead.
    Most organizers either switch platforms or pay it without
    complaint. Both outcomes are fine.

Q3: How much prep is actually required?
A3: For the entry Stump Jerry's Brains session: zero. The Brain is
    always current. Jerry opens the tool and starts navigating. For
    the Custom Keynote: about 30 minutes of node-review. No slides,
    no script, no rehearsal. This is a feature — the session's energy
    comes precisely from the fact that it's genuinely live.

Q4: What if the audience picks a topic that isn't in the Brain?
A4: That's often the most interesting moment. With 500K+ nodes, most
    topics are in there. But occasionally something isn't — and
    watching Jerry navigate around the gap, find adjacencies, and
    explain what the Brain's edges reveal is frequently more
    illuminating than finding it directly.

Q5: What platforms does this work on?
A5: Any platform where Jerry can share his screen: Zoom, Hopin,
    Airmeet, StreamYard, Riverside, and most custom virtual event
    platforms. Jerry's Brain also has a public web version at
    jerrysbrain.com that can be navigated directly in a browser.

Q6: Can sessions be recorded?
A6: Yes. Recordings can be provided to organizers after the event and
    shared with attendees. Jerry requests a courtesy copy for his own
    archive. They hold up well because watching the navigation unfold
    is the experience, not just the information.

Q7: What is "Brain on Call"?
A7: An ongoing retainer for organizations that want Jerry's
    sensemaking over time — regular navigation sessions, pattern
    recognition, and ad hoc access when a hard question needs a
    connected mind. Introduced after a live session when fit is clear.
