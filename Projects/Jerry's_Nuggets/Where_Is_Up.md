# Where is "up"? 

*You should be able to follow the bubbles.* 

Sometimes in Scuba diving, you get disoriented. You might be in a cave, where every wall looks alike, or deep enough where it's dark and you can't tell where you are. Doing the wrong thing at those moments is life-threatening. Divers have a great saying for situations like this: "Always trust the bubbles." They will always show you the way to the surface. 

One of my favorite aspects of TheBrain software is that it has a sense of orientation, a feeling of where "up" is. Compare this Brain screen: 

![[JB - Bubbles.jpg]]

With this "rubber band" diagram, where you can't really tell what is where. It's almost illegible. 

![[do-you-see-an-advantage-in-3d-mindmapping-compared-to-a-v0-92zshbbmzqx91.jpeg]]

Okay, maybe that one's unfair; how about this one?

![[knowledge graphs messy mind map.jpg]]

Many mind maps are attractive, but relatively useless. In 2005, Manuel Lima started a website called [VisualComplexity.com](http://www.visualcomplexity.com), which went viral (but appears to have turned into a book site about visualization). Early on, though, it was a gallery of cool visualizations. You can get a feel for it from [this Wayback capture](https://web.archive.org/web/20120705192354/http://www.visualcomplexity.com/vc/). 

One of the problems I had with the site was that a majority of the examples it highlighted weren't very useful. They were just flashy. And unfortunately, visualizations can often get by on being flashy. The problems arise when you try to navigate or curate them. When you start to make them practical, few pass the test.
