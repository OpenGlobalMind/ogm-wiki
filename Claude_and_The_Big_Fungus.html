The Big Fungus: A Conversation

Jerry Michalski & Claude · April 3, 2026

This document captures the first part of a long conversation that began with Humberto Maturana and ended up somewhere much larger — a working theory of what Jerry's Brain is for, what the Big Fungus could become, and how those projects relate to Jerry's life and work. The conversation then shifted into operational work on Stump Jerry's Brains. This document preserves the strategic and philosophical thread before it gets buried.

Return to this. It matters.

I. Maturana's Core Ideas

The conversation started with a briefing on Humberto Maturana (1928–2021), the Chilean biologist whose work with Francisco Varela reshaped ideas about life, cognition, and what it means to know anything.

Autopoiesis is the foundation — the idea that living systems are self-producing, self-maintaining networks. A cell, an organism, a mind: each continuously regenerates its own parts to preserve its unity. Life must change to stay the same.

The radical move: cognition isn't something that happens in a brain. It is life. Living systems are cognitive systems, and living as a process is a process of cognition.

Structural coupling is how living systems interact with their environment. The environment doesn't instruct an organism — perturbations from outside trigger changes determined by the organism's own internal structure. You don't download reality; you co-construct it through ongoing interaction. The history of those interactions shapes what you can perceive and think.

The observer is central and inescapable. Any scientist studying a system is part of the system's reality. Descriptions of nature always involve the language and perspective of the observer. There is no view from nowhere.

Bringing forth a world — Maturana's phrase for what this adds up to. Cognition is not a representation of the world "out there" but an ongoing bringing forth of a world through the process of living itself. And this world-bringing-forth happens with others, in language.

Languaging (his verb form) is not a symbol system for transmitting information. It coordinates action and attention. Language doesn't carry meaning across a gap; it shapes what becomes visible in a shared space.

Love — which sounds surprising in a biologist — is his word for the foundational condition of social existence. Without love, without acceptance of others living beside us, there is no social process and therefore no humanness. Anything that undermines the acceptance of others — from competency to the possession of truth to ideological certainty — undermines the social process because it undermines the biological process that generates it.

Key texts: The Tree of Knowledge (1987, with Varela) is the accessible entry point. Autopoiesis and Cognition (1980) is the denser foundational work. His later Biology of Love pieces are where the ethical and social implications are most explicit.

II. The Extended Mind Thesis

Andy Clark and David Chalmers (1998) asked: "Where does the mind stop and the rest of the world begin?" Their answer: cognitive processes "ain't all in the head."

The core move: objects within the environment function as part of the mind. The mind and the environment act as a "coupled system" that can be seen as a complete cognitive system of its own.

The famous test case: Otto has Alzheimer's and carries a notebook with directions. Inga recalls the same information from biological memory. Clark and Chalmers argue there is no fundamental difference between Inga and Otto, memory and notebook. If a process would count as cognitive when it happens inside the skull, it counts as cognitive when it happens outside the skull — provided the coupling is tight enough.

The criterion for "tight enough": the external resource must be reliably accessible, automatically consulted, and its outputs directly endorsed by the person.

Where EMT and Maturana agree: Both reject the "skin and skull" boundary as the definitive limit of cognition. The boundaries of mind are functional, not anatomical.

Where they diverge: Clark and Chalmers are asking an ontological question — where is the mind? Maturana is asking a biological one — what is cognition as a living process? Maturana would find the Otto/Inga framing too static. The notebook is a passive repository Otto accesses. But structural coupling is dynamic and bidirectional — the environment doesn't just store things for you, it shapes you over time, and you shape it. The relationship has a history that transforms both parties.

Another tension: Maturana is deeply skeptical of the idea that information is "in" things. For him, there's no information transferred from environment to organism — only perturbations that trigger internally-determined responses.

Key texts: Clark's Supersizing the Mind (2008) is more accessible than the original 1998 paper and engages with exactly this kind of deep coupling.

III. Jerry's Brain Through These Lenses

Jerry has been building and maintaining a public personal knowledge graph in TheBrain software for nearly three decades — over 625,000 nodes as of 2026.

Through the Clark/Chalmers lens: The Brain is straightforwardly an extended mind. It isn't a tool Jerry uses; it's part of how Jerry thinks. Querying it is more like consulting long-term memory than doing a Google search. Importantly: from the perspective of the Extended Mind Thesis, interfering with such a system raises the same ethical issues as interfering with someone's brain.

Through the Maturana lens: But the Brain goes further than Clark and Chalmers' static notebook. It has been a site of structural coupling for three decades. Jerry hasn't just stored things in it — he's been shaped by the act of curating it. The way he notices things, the distinctions he makes, the connections he's drawn to — all of this has been co-evolved with the Brain over time. It's closer to the organism-niche relationship Maturana describes than to Otto's notebook.

The synthesis for Human Provenance: The EMT explains why the Brain is cognitively significant. Maturana explains why it's irreplaceable. The Brain isn't just an external memory store — it's the crystallized record of a specific observer's 30-year history of structural coupling with ideas. An AI can query the Brain's nodes and links. It cannot replicate the observer who created them, because that observer and that Brain have been constituting each other over time.

The Human Provenance argument isn't merely biographical. It has deep theoretical grounding in both frameworks.

IV. Design from Trust

Maturana's grounding of ethics and cooperation in love (acceptance of others) is a biological argument for exactly the kind of relational design Jerry has been developing as "Design from Trust."

Anything that undermines the acceptance of others — from competency to the possession of truth — undermines the social process. That's a systems-biology argument for why control and surveillance damage the very thing they're trying to protect.

The Extended Mind Thesis adds something parallel: if our cognitive systems genuinely extend into our tools and our relationships with other people, then environments designed around surveillance and control aren't just unpleasant — they're cognitively amputating. Interactions with other people are crucial elements of your environment and cognitive process.

Both frameworks converge on the same conclusion: trust isn't just an ethical preference. It's a cognitive infrastructure requirement.

V. The Big Fungus — A Working Theory

Jerry described a larger vision he half-jokingly calls "The Big Fungus": a space where people using very different tools for thinking can compare notes, make arguments, and eventually evolve more useful ways of building society and stewarding places.

Key characteristics of the vision:

  • No copyright, no paywall, no prevention of free access
  • The Brain as a foundational node, not the whole thing
  • Interoperability between different thinking tools (Roam, Obsidian, TheBrain, Tinderbox, and others)
  • Conversations that leave the shared territory better mapped than they found it
  • The mycelial metaphor: fungi don't have a center; they transfer nutrients between organisms that couldn't exchange them directly

The Bannon scenario — a recurrent use case in Jerry's mind: taking notes on an interview with someone whose views differ sharply from his own, then having a structured conversation in which he navigates related issues in the Brain, steelmans the other position if needed, and leaves the whole thing more useful at the end than at the beginning. Each conversation should simplify or complexify and improve the Fungus, so that more people can come feed off it — the way fungi feed farmer ants.

What the Big Fungus actually needs: The problem that has prevented this from existing isn't technical. Tools like RDF and the semantic web tried the technical route for decades and largely failed. The problem is trust and incentive: why would someone open up their thinking to a commons? What do they get back? Jerry's answer: you get back a richer map than any one person can make alone, and you don't have to monetize it because the value is in the thinking itself, not in controlling access.

The core project underneath all of this: A method for productive encounters between different minds and maps — where "productive" means the shared territory gets better mapped as a result, and the improvement is durable and accessible to others. The Brain is the most developed instance of this method. Stump Jerry's Brain is a live demo of the method in conversation with an audience. The Bannon scenario is the method pushed into adversarial, high-stakes territory.

What's missing isn't the vision. What's missing is a name and a legible description of the method itself.

VI. The Nested Projects

These projects nest inside each other rather than sitting side by side:

  • Embiggenment — the largest frame: a personal and relational process Jerry and April are on, mostly separately and also together, as the things they do and care about converge.
  • Next Stacks — the civilizational ambition: identifying high-functioning elements of discourse and decision-making that are so good they become contagious and begin displacing dysfunctional systems. One element might be Open Space format meetings. The secret wish: these elements replace the consumer-mass-market systems we have today.
  • Big Fungus — the infrastructure that would make Next Stacks possible: a substrate for productive encounters between different minds and maps.
  • Brain Legacy — the personal contribution Jerry already holds: making the curation method legible, building a posse, potentially creating JerrysBrainBot, ensuring continuity after Jerry.
  • Stump Jerry's Brains — the revenue engine and live practice: funds everything else, keeps Jerry sharp.

On civic engagement: Jerry subscribes to Beyond Intractability (Heidi and Guy Burgess) and cares about the dysfunction of civic/political projects that are temporary and separate from real lives. The question he poses: why can't important issues like how we zone or design a street be lively, ongoing conversations easily read and adopted by other communities around the world — rather than one-time votes? The Big Fungus is one answer to this.

VII. The Three Things That Follow

1. The legibility project as method document. Not just "here's how to navigate my Brain" but "here's what I've learned about thinking in public with an external map, and what becomes possible when you do it for decades." This is the intellectual contribution that justifies everything else. It earns Jerry a seat at the Knowledge Futures table. A short interview series — with Claude and a friend — is a good way to surface it.

2. The Externalizers cohort as method community. Very few intense tool-for-thought users are Externalizers. Most are just building resources for themselves. The interesting group: people who have made their thinking public and navigable by others over a long period. A tiny, weird, valuable group. The conversation between them should be about what they've each learned from long-term externalization, not about comparing maps directly.

3. The LLM bridge as proof of concept. If Jerry's Brain, with his observer's perspective baked in as context, produces meaningfully different conversations than a generic LLM — and it would — that's a demonstration that human-curated externalizations add something that can't be replicated by training on the whole internet. TheBrain v15 now has AI deeply embedded in the UI, currently invoking ChatGPT. Claude is next on their agenda. The ideal: the Claude invoked by TheBrain is Jerry's specific Claude account, rather than a generic one trained on curating Brains.

VIII. On Continuity — Jerry's Brain After Jerry

A few years ago, some OGM community members held a Zoom call titled "Jerry's Brain after Jerry." One insight: Jerry needs a posse that knows how to extend his Brain if it is to continue expanding after his death.

The problem breaks into levels:

Technical (easiest): TheBrain's format, export capabilities, and LLMs working with structured knowledge graphs mean the data can survive and remain navigable.

Curatorial (harder): The Brain's value isn't in its nodes — it's in the distinctions behind the connections. Why did Jerry link these two things? What was he noticing when he made that connection in 1998 versus 2019? A posse needs to have absorbed enough of Jerry's observer's perspective to make new connections that are faithful to it. This is closer to an apprenticeship than a technical handoff.

Identity (deepest): At some point "Jerry's Brain after Jerry" stops being Jerry's Brain and becomes something else — a commons seeded by Jerry's Brain. That transition needs to be designed, not just allowed to happen. The mycelium outlasts any individual fruiting body. The question is whether the network has enough of its own integrity to keep growing without the original organism.

A possible path: JerrysBrainBot — an LLM trained on or given access to Jerry's Brain as context, able to adopt Jerry's approach to curation more easily and consistently than any human apprentice. Not a replacement for human continuity, but a different kind of stewardship.

IX. Key Tensions Worth Returning To

  1. Openness vs. legibility. The Brain has been public for years and usage is uneven. The move from publicly available to actively useful to others requires intentional shaping. Making choices about what to foreground and how to invite people in isn't a compromise of openness — it's completing the gesture.

  2. Ambition vs. execution. Jerry has a history of visions more ambitious than any one person should undertake, combined with difficulty estimating how long things take. The projects most likely to actually happen are the ones that can make meaningful progress in the time naturally given before something else captures attention.

  3. Revenue vs. the larger work. Stump Jerry's Brains needs to be the near-term priority because it funds everything else. But the legibility project, the Externalizers cohort, and the LLM bridge aren't separate projects — they're the same work at different scales.

  4. Pride of authorship. Jerry noted that until recently, pride of authorship was constraining him — wanting every post to be his own words, every Brain entry placed by him. He's rethought this. The shift matters: it means AI collaboration can genuinely accelerate the work rather than just assist it.

X. A Note on This Conversation Itself

This conversation is itself an instance of the method. Jerry brought his Brain, his history, his observer's perspective. The encounter produced distinctions that neither party would have arrived at alone. It left the territory better mapped than it found it.

That's worth noticing. And worth returning to.

Next time: pick up the threads above before diving into operations. The Big Fungus, the legibility project, the Next Stacks, the Externalizers cohort — these are not separate from the work of building SJB. They are the reason the work matters.
